I used to hate stopping.

Stopping felt like losing ground. Like while I stood still, everyone else was getting further ahead. Momentum was everything. Keep moving, keep building, keep going — even when the direction was wrong.

That's the trap nobody warns you about.

You can be incredibly busy going nowhere. You can be *efficient* at the wrong thing. You can work yourself to the bone on a path that — if you stopped for ten minutes and looked at it honestly — you'd recognize as someone else's map.

## The Campfire Principle

I spend time outdoors when I can. There's something a campfire does to your thinking that nothing else replicates. It slows you down. Not because it forces you to — but because you let it. You sit there and you just... watch it.

That's stopping. Not giving up. Not quitting. Just creating enough stillness to see what's actually in front of you.

Most people are afraid of that stillness. Because in the stillness, the honest questions show up.

*Is this what I want?*  
*Is this working?*  
*Who told me this was the right direction — and did they have my best interests in mind?*

## The Stop Is the Strategy

Here's what I know now: the stop isn't the pause before the pivot. **The stop IS the pivot.** It's the moment you reclaim agency.

Life pushes. Jobs push. Expectations push. Culture pushes. People push. And most of us, without realizing it, spend years being pushed.

Stop. Think. Pivot isn't a motivational slogan. It's a sequence. And it only works if you actually do the first one.
